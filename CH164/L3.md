## 2. Ionic bonding
- Molecules or atoms gain or lose electron to form ions
- Forming bonds between **oppositely charged** cations and anions.
- **Non-directional**, unlike covalent bond
### 2.1. Lattices
- Cations and anions pack tightly and regularly to from lattices.
	- The large amount of electrostatic interactions stabilise the salts
- Lattice enthalpy varies greatly, between 600 - 4000 kJ/mol
	- Strongest lattice enthalpies are formed when there is a **large ion size mismatch**, or when the **charges are very high**.
	- The energy of *one* ionic electrostatic interaction can be ~200 kJ/mol as gas, but only ~1-8 kJ/mol in water, as solvents can weaken the electrostatic interactions.
		- The weakening ability of solvents is measured by its **dielectric constant**
	- Lattice enthalpies can be used to **estimate solubility** by comparing **lattice enthalpy to enthalpy of solvation.**
	- Lattice enthalpies are used in **Born-Haber** cycles to calculate other enthalpies such as atomisation, ionisation, and electron affinity.
## 3. Metallic bonding
- Metal atoms' electrons delocalise to from a 'sea of electrons' and metal cations. 
- Their electrostatic interaction stabilises the metal.
- The number of electrons that delocalises can be seen from melting points.
	- Metals with high melting points typically lose the most electrons and are also very highly charged, e.g. Tungsten (W).
## 4. Intermolecular interactions
- Three types:
	- Permanent dipole-Permanent dipole (e.g. hydrogen bonding)
	- Permanent dipole-Induced dipole 
	- Temporary dipole-Induced dipole, aka London dispersion forces, weak.
- The overall interaction arises from a combination of all three types
- Magnitude decreases with $\frac{1}{R^6}$. R is the distance between atoms. 
### 4.1. London dispersion forces
- Characterised by quantum mechanics.
- Heavier elements are more polarisable, hence stronger London dispersion forces.
	- He bp = 4 K
	- Xe bp = 166 K due to more London dispersion forces.
	- In non-polar molecules the intermolecular forces are 100% dispersion.
	- Polar molecules have a mix of everything.
		- Water is ~35% dispersion, the rest is dipoles.
### 4.2. Lennard-Jones Potential
- As electrons are negatively charged, atoms (and consequently molecules) will repel each other. 
- So we must consider the balance of these forces.
	- To do this we can calculate the **Lennard-Jones Potential

- Repulsion weakens even quicker at $\frac{1}{R^{12}}$, while only starting at very short distances.
